# Website Review: Current Employee Perspective

**Reviewer:** Marcus Powell
**Role:** Senior Consultant
**Tenure:** 3 years with Solutions Delivered
**Location:** Birmingham, UK
**Review Date:** November 20, 2025
**Review Type:** Internal Employee Experience Assessment

---

## Executive Summary

After three years with Solutions Delivered, I'm conflicted about our website. On one hand, it's professionally designed, accessible, and presents our services clearly to potential clients. On the other hand, it provides almost no value to me as an employee, and more concerning, it doesn't reflect the actual company I work for.

When clients ask me for materials to share, I'm left scrambling. When I want to point my professional network to our website to show what we're achieving, there's nothing there. When I need to explain what the company is doing to grow and evolve, the website offers no evidence. Most frustratingly, the external brand doesn't match the internal culture I experience daily.

The website feels like a brochure that was created once and then forgotten. It serves new customer acquisition reasonably well, but completely fails everyone else: current employees, existing clients, partners, and anyone trying to understand what Solutions Delivered actually does beyond generic service descriptions.

**Overall Rating: 4/10**

It works as a basic marketing site, but falls short of what employees need to represent the company effectively and what we need to feel genuinely proud of our workplace.

---

## Strengths

### 1. Professional Design and Accessibility
I can feel proud of the technical quality. The site is WCAG 2.2 compliant, which aligns with the accessibility-first approach we take in our client work. The design is clean, modern, and professional. When clients look us up, they see a competent, quality-focused organization.

### 2. Clear Service Descriptions
The Solutions page does a solid job explaining our four core service areas. When I'm talking to a potential client and they want to understand what we offer, I can confidently point them to this page. The descriptions are accurate and reflect the work we actually do.

### 3. Accurate "How We Work" Content
The How We Work page genuinely reflects our collaborative approach. As someone who lives this process daily, I appreciate that the external messaging matches our internal methodology. New clients coming in have accurate expectations.

### 4. Values Alignment
The About page articulates values (Integrity, Collaboration, Excellence) that I actually see in practice. Unlike many corporate websites, these aren't just marketing fluff—they're values I witness in how we work with clients and each other.

### 5. Quality Commitment
The "no-bloat philosophy" and focus on tailored solutions accurately represents our approach to web development. This resonates with my experience and makes it easier to explain our differentiation to prospects.

---

## Weaknesses

### 1. **CRITICAL: No Employee Resources**
After three years, I still have nowhere to go on our own website for:
- Brand assets (logos, style guides, templates)
- Presentation templates for client meetings
- Case study materials to share with prospects
- Company announcements or news
- Internal policies or procedures
- Employee handbook
- Benefits information
- Holiday calendars
- Training resources

I'm constantly asking colleagues for files that should be centrally available. This is embarrassing for a consultancy that specializes in process optimization.

### 2. **No Case Studies or Success Stories**
This is my biggest frustration as a consultant. When I'm meeting with a prospect who asks "Can you show me an example of similar work?" I have nothing to point to. I either:
- Verbally describe previous projects (which lacks credibility)
- Create my own ad-hoc materials (inconsistent and time-consuming)
- Ask the delivery team for project details (burdening others)

We've delivered excellent work over the years—where is it? Why can't I share our successes? This makes my job significantly harder and makes me question whether leadership values our achievements.

### 3. **No Company News or Blog**
How do I show prospects that we're innovative and staying current? There's no evidence of:
- Recent projects or achievements
- Industry thought leadership
- New service offerings
- Company growth or milestones
- Team updates or announcements

When clients ask "What's new at Solutions Delivered?" I can only talk about what I personally know. The website offers no support for demonstrating our evolution and expertise.

### 4. **Missing Team Information**
Where are my colleagues? There's no:
- Team directory or profiles
- Leadership team information
- Consultant bios and expertise areas
- Contact information for different teams
- Organizational structure

When I want to introduce a client to a colleague with specific expertise, I can't point them to a team page. It makes us seem like a one-person operation rather than a growing consultancy.

### 5. **No Career Development Information**
The Careers page is generic and tells me nothing about:
- Internal career paths or progression opportunities
- Professional development programs
- Training and certification support
- Internal mobility options
- How the company invests in employee growth

When I'm asked by contacts interested in joining, I can't point them to detailed career information. When I'm considering my own future here, the website offers no insight into growth opportunities.

### 6. **Brand Inconsistency with Internal Reality**
The website presents a professional but somewhat generic consultancy. The Solutions Delivered I work for is:
- More innovative than the site suggests
- More personable and collaborative
- More technical and hands-on
- More values-driven
- More connected to the communities we serve

The gap between our external brand and internal culture concerns me. It suggests we don't really know how to articulate what makes us special.

### 7. **No Social Proof or Credibility Indicators**
When justifying our rates or defending our approach, I need evidence:
- Client testimonials
- Industry certifications or partnerships
- Awards or recognition
- Client logos or references
- Project statistics or outcomes

None of this exists. I'm left relying on personal credibility rather than organizational proof points.

### 8. **No Content Worth Sharing Professionally**
I'm active on LinkedIn and want to share company content with my network. But there's nothing to share:
- No blog posts about industry insights
- No project showcases
- No thought leadership articles
- No webinars or events
- No downloadable resources

I end up sharing competitors' content because they're publishing useful material. This is frustrating and feels like a missed opportunity for both the company and my professional brand.

### 9. **No Client Resources**
When I'm working with a client and want to point them to supporting materials, there's nothing:
- No best practice guides
- No methodology documentation
- No frameworks or templates
- No FAQs or troubleshooting guides
- No "how to work with us" resources

I have to create these materials myself or dig through past project files. This is inefficient and leads to inconsistency across client engagements.

### 10. **No Evidence of Community Involvement**
We do good work in the community and care about making a positive impact, but the website shows none of this. There's no mention of:
- Community partnerships
- Charitable activities
- Pro bono work
- Sustainability initiatives
- Local business support

I want to feel proud of our community contribution, but I can't point to any evidence of it publicly.

## Summary and Final Thoughts

I care deeply about Solutions Delivered. I've spent three years here building relationships, developing expertise, and delivering quality work for clients. The company culture is genuinely collaborative, the work is meaningful, and I feel valued by my colleagues and leadership.

**But you wouldn't know any of that from our website.**

The disconnect bothers me for several reasons:

**First, it makes my job harder.** When I'm trying to close a deal and a prospect asks for examples of our work, I have nothing to share. When I want to introduce a client to a colleague with specific expertise, there's no team directory. When I need a presentation template or brand assets, I have to ask around. These are basic resources that should be readily available.

**Second, it limits my professional growth.** I'm proud of the work we do, but I can't easily share it with my LinkedIn network. There's no thought leadership content from our team, no blog posts I can comment on, no company achievements I can celebrate publicly. This makes it harder for me to build my personal brand and professional reputation.

**Third, it raises questions about leadership priorities.** We tell clients we specialize in process optimization and digital transformation, yet our own digital presence is minimal and clearly hasn't been prioritized. That inconsistency is awkward to explain when prospects notice it.

**Fourth, it affects my pride in the company.** When I tell someone I work for Solutions Delivered and they look us up, they see a generic consultancy website with no personality, no team, and no evidence of success. That doesn't reflect the innovative, collaborative, impactful company I experience every day.

**The reality is:** Solutions Delivered is better than this website suggests. We're doing impressive work, we have talented people, we're growing and evolving, and we genuinely care about our clients and our impact. None of that comes through on the website.

**This matters for recruitment too.** When I recommend Solutions Delivered to talented colleagues who are job hunting, they visit the website and see nothing compelling about career development, company culture, or what makes us different. The generic careers page doesn't sell the opportunity effectively.

**What needs to change:**

1. **Invest in case studies.** We've been doing great work—let's showcase it. This is table stakes for any consultancy and it's embarrassing that we don't have them.

2. **Create employee resources.** Give us the tools we need: brand assets, templates, case studies, contact info, and guidance. Stop making us recreate the wheel.

3. **Show the team.** We're talented people doing interesting work. Let's be visible and human, not a faceless corporate entity.

4. **Start publishing.** Blog posts, articles, insights—anything that demonstrates our expertise and gives employees content worth sharing.

5. **Reflect our culture.** The website should capture the innovation, collaboration, and impact that defines our work, not just list our services.

**I'll continue recommending Solutions Delivered to prospects because I believe in what we do.** But I'd feel much prouder and more confident if our website actually reflected the quality of our company and gave me the resources I need to represent us effectively.

**To leadership:** The website is an investment in employee effectiveness, not just marketing. When you give us the tools to succeed—case studies, resources, brand assets—you multiply the impact of every consultant. When you showcase our achievements publicly, you validate our efforts and make recruitment easier. When you demonstrate thought leadership, you elevate all of our professional brands.

**The website should work for employees, not just prospects.** Please consider investing in it as an employee resource and effectiveness tool, not just a marketing brochure.
